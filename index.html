Living Well in Europe

What Shapes Everyday Well-Being Across European Countries

The continent of Europe is typically characterized as a stable place with long life expectancy rates, established public systems, and a somewhat predictable political and economic structure. However, the experience of everyday life can differ significantly from one European country to another.

In some places, people report a general sense of confidence in daily life. Public systems are expected to function, institutions are largely trusted, corruption low, and future prospects appear manageable. In other contexts, daily experiences are more constrained, shaped by economic pressure, uncertainty, and a weaker confidence in social and institutional arrangements.

These differences cannot be explained by economic output alone. Income is often associated with well-being, yet this pattern is not consistent among countries with similar resources. This suggests that perceived well-being depends not only on economic conditions, but also on how those conditions are experienced in everyday life — through security, social connection, and a sense of autonomy.

What matters, then, is the context that shapes these experiences. Differences in social and institutional contexts affect the extent to which people feel secure, supported, and in control in their daily lives. Receiving support in difficult situations, trusting public institutions, feeling in control of one's life decisions, and the strength of social networks are all part of the picture.

The data that follows explores these patterns across Europe, revealing where confidence and security shape daily life, and where uncertainty and pressure dominate the experience.

Scope: This analysis examines EU member states, EFTA countries (Iceland, Liechtenstein, Norway, Switzerland), and the United Kingdom. Data drawn from the World Happiness Report 2025, Our World in Data, and the World Bank. Full sources provided for each visualization.

How Europeans Feel About Life

Well-being should not be seen as a fluctuating mood state. Instead, well-being has slow-moving, long-term dimensions.

When people are asked to take a global view and judge their lives as a whole, they tend to rely primarily on stable, long-term features such as stability, relationships, health, trust, and opportunities.

The following map represents this global self-evaluation across Europe. The focus here is not on short-term changes — such as an especially good day or a bad week — but on a long-term snapshot of the general life in a country, which emerges when the surrounding "noise" recedes and people are asked the seemingly simple but actually revealing question: "How's your life going?"

Source: World Happiness Report (2025), Figure 2.1

How to read this map?

Countries are coloured by average life satisfaction on a 0–10 scale, where 0 is the worst possible life and 10 is the best possible.

Map colours are scaled to the range of observed countries (5.6—7.7) for improved visual contrast — darker blues indicate relatively higher satisfaction within Europe.

Think of it as a national "temperature check" for lived experience: not what a country promises, but how people say it actually feels to live there.
The top spots in the ranking illustrate a well-established trend: Finland, Denmark, Iceland, Sweden, and Norway occupy five of the top seven spots, and this can be explained by decades-long investment in institutional trust and public systems, creating an environment that supports a pervasive feeling of routine security.

Southern Europe offers a different story.

Social life appears to be rich and closely connected. Family dinners go late into the evening, people know each other in the neighborhood, and town squares are inhabited on a warm summer nights.

Yet Spain, Italy, Portugal, and Greece are found in the lower third of European well-being tables. Economic pressures and insecurity are dominant features here, with high rates of youth unemployment, insecure work, and lingering effects of recent debt crises.

Community may provide security, but it does not pay the rent.

In Central and Eastern Europe, the picture is mixed. Today, countries that have had a similar past have clearly divergent well-being outcomes.

Lithuania ranks 11th overall, while Latvia ranks 25th, a difference of 0.6 points, even though they have had a similar past under the Soviets and are both in the European Union. Having had a similar past does not guarantee a similar present or future.

The biggest lesson is also the most inconvenient: money isn't a shortcut to a good life. Some high-income countries rank lower than one expects, suggesting that comfort and contentment are often similar in theory, but not always so in practice.

France, the United Kingdom, and Belgium have a similar level of prosperity, yet flop against some countries with less economic wealth. Such an observation calls for a consideration of what money cannot buy — a question the next chapters explore in depth.

Wealth and Well-Being

If you line European countries up by income, the pattern looks reassuring:
more money equals higher well-being. It reads like a clear equation.

This chapter tests how far that equation holds. We examine whether income reliably predicts how people evaluate their lives, and where the relationship weakens, breaks, or becomes more complicated once countries are compared more closely.

How strongly is income linked to well-being?

Across countries, higher income is generally associated with higher life evaluation. On average, people in wealthier societies tend to rate their lives more positively.

But the simplicity does not survive a closer look. Among countries with similar income levels, differences in well-being are large enough to raise questions. Economic prosperity does not translate into the same lived experience everywhere.

How income is measured in this chart

Income is measured as gross domestic product per capita, adjusted for purchasing power parity. This adjustment accounts for differences in the cost of living across countries, where the same amount of money can translate into very different material conditions.

To reduce the influence of short-term economic fluctuations, income values are averaged across the years 2022 to 2024. This highlights more stable differences between countries rather than temporary shocks.

Life evaluation reflects how people rate their own lives on a scale from zero to ten using the Cantril ladder. Within this group of countries, scores cluster within a relatively narrow range. The y-axis is therefore limited to highlight meaningful variation, rather than stretching the scale across values that do not appear in the data.

High income often aligns with higher well-being

Across Europe, countries with higher income levels generally report higher life evaluation. This is particularly true for countries located in the northern and western parts of the European continent.

On the surface, this relationship is a positive and reassuring phenomenon, as it suggests that money is being used for its intended purpose.

Similar money, different outcomes

In this case, several high-income countries cluster closely in economic terms. Their GDP levels sit within a narrow range, close enough to invite direct comparison.

And yet, their reported well-being does not move in lockstep. Some countries feel noticeably better to live in than others, even when income offers little help in telling them apart.

This explanation cannot be found in bank balances. The following chapter will explore the social and institutional factors that may affect this translation, with a focus on support, trust, and confidence in public systems.

What Actually Builds Well-Being?

Income matters, but it does not account for all differences in well-being. Countries with similar levels of prosperity can still show substantial variation in how people evaluate their lives.

This chapter examines factors associated with well-being, starting from income and extending beyond it. The following steps introduce these conditions one at a time, such as social support, health, freedom, trust, and generosity. Together, they show how similar economic resources can lead to different outcomes.

What income makes possible

Income affects well-being mainly through its impact on conditions experienced every day. It affects the degree to which people can meet their needs, protect themselves from financial or health-related shocks, and stabilize their everyday lives.

Income does not produce well-being; instead, it increases the range of possible conditions, such as having a stable place to live, access to healthcare, freedom to change jobs, or having time for leisure.

In the chart, income is shown as one of the factors that can impact the overall life evaluation score. It shows the amount of total outcome that is statistically linked to income, as opposed to income levels themselves.

Where income may reach its limits

Here, countries are ranked in terms of their income contribution to their overall life evaluation score. Only income data is included.

Income contribution across countries varies, though within a relatively narrow range. No country stands out as being dominated by income.

This indicates that, while income may be important, it is unlikely to be driving differences between countries in their overall score.

Does support change how income is experienced?

Social support shrinks into one profound question that carries real weight: if life hits you hard, do you have someone you can rely on?

That person could be a family member, a friend, or a neighbor, the everyday kind of help that carries people through tough moments.

Having this support can help shape how manageable everyday struggles seem, even when money and resources are tight.

Similar incomes, different buffers

When social support is stronger, people feel less exposed. Money stretches further when you're not facing everything alone.

When that support is absent, even economic comfort will offer less protection, and small setbacks can compound into a sustained sense of vulnerability and pressure.

Does living longer translate into feeling better?

Healthy life expectancy is not simply about adding years. It reflects how much of life is spent without persistent limitations, the difference between staying alive and actually being able to live.

The difference matters. Extra years lived in poor health do little to improve how people evaluate their lives, while years lived with autonomy and physical capacity to participate in day-to-day activities tend to matter much more.

Health as a condition, not a conclusion

Health is a secure foundation upon which well-being is built, and countries in which people have longer and healthier lives are rarely found at the bottom of life evaluations. Good health reduces life's constraints, which otherwise subtract from well-being.

While health is a major determinant, it does not fully explain why some high-performing countries score higher than others. Even when health outcomes are broadly similar, life evaluations can still diverge notably. This suggests that once basic functioning is secured, the remaining differences are less about survival and more about how safe, supported, and empowered everyday life feels.

Do people feel able to shape their own lives?

Perceived freedom reflects whether people feel able to make meaningful choices about their own lives, be it work, relationships, direction, and identity.

Economic security does not necessarily mean having a sense of personal autonomy in certain situations. The absence of a perceived personal control in one's life means economic security may feel impressive on paper, but underwhelming in practice.

What's the point of having a good-paying job if you feel trapped in it? What good is a big home if you feel stuck there?

Resources without agency

If people perceive that they have little ability to shape important decisions in their lives, their well-being tends to suffer even in conditions that are relatively favorable in other respects.

Perceived autonomy affects people's approaches to their jobs, their interpersonal relations, and their plans for the future, and hence should be regarded as a central rather than a peripheral condition.

Can everyday life feel secure without trust?

Trust means the expectation of fairness in the behavior of other people and the perception of institutions as reliable.

Lack of trust creates friction in various areas of life, and this friction translates into greater stress levels, cynicism, and the perception that life is harder than it has to be.

Why trust makes life feel easier

High levels of trust also correlate with increased predictability in one's life. Trust is far from perfect but is related to perceptions of fairness, calmness, and minimal mental relaxation.

In these settings, people spend less time struggling with or doubting the trustworthiness of systems and more time focusing on their daily activities.

What everyday generosity might signal

In the decomposition, the influence of generosity is smaller relative to most other factors. The numerical weight is small.

What it actually measures is the degree to which providing assistance is common and anticipated, and thus serves as a proxy for social norms and cooperation.

Signals of social cohesion

Higher generosity scores tend to appear in places where social ties feel stronger and more balanced.

Although generosity does not necessarily explain levels of well-being, it often appears in contexts where cooperative and supportive societies are assumed or taken for granted.

What remains unexplained

Even after accounting for income, health, social support, freedom, trust, and generosity, differences in how people evaluate their lives remain.

The final segment of this section represents the portion of well-being that cannot be explained by these factors. It represents factors that are more difficult to quantify, such as expectations, cultural influences, recent events, and attitudes.

Feeling better or worse than expected

When this remaining component is considered, some countries appear to perform better than their measured circumstances suggest.

While others underachieve. On paper, they have what should be a good life, yet people report something missing.

Collectively, these trends suggest that a simple, yet compelling, truth underlies all of the trends presented in this chapter: no one factor in particular is sufficient to explain well-being in isolation. While income, social support, health, freedom, trust, and generosity are all associated with well-being, none of these factors dominates or fully explains the others.

In fact, societies that achieve similar levels of overall well-being do so in different combinations of these factors. In some cases, economic security may dominate, while in other cases, social support, trust in institutions, or personal autonomy may offset weaker performances in other areas.

In the end, what matters most is not that one or another factor dominates, but how these factors interact with one another. Well-being is ultimately a product of their interaction, shaped by social and institutional context as much as by economic or individual factors.

How Well-Being in Europe Has Changed Over Time

Well-being does not stand still. Over time, it shifts, sometimes slowly and sometimes more abruptly.

The current chapter directly deals with these dynamics of well-being over time. It uses a simple timeline approach to examine the change in average life evaluation over time, with an emphasis on overall patterns rather than specific details.

The focus of the analysis is on change rather than rank. In cross-country comparisons, phases of stability, gradual change, and more dramatic change emerge.

Over time, countries take very different paths. Some show stability, some show gradual change, and some show more dramatic change, including change large enough to alter their relative positions.

Source: Our World in Data (2025), based on data from the Wellbeing Research Centre; Eurostat, OECD, IMF, and World Bank.

Each line represents a country. Its position indicates an average life evaluation, and how that position shifts over time reveals whether well-being is stable, rising, or falling.

At the start of the timeline, countries already differ noticeably. Average life evaluation begins at clearly different levels, with some countries starting much higher than others.

As time progresses, many of these positions begin to shift. Several countries move upward, while others remain stable or decline, gradually changing the overall distribution.

Over longer periods, these shifts become more consistent. Short-term ups and downs fade into the background, and longer-term tendencies begin to stand out.

The life evaluation of a small group of countries, including Austria, Germany, Switzerland, the UK, and Cyprus, continues with an increasing trend of decline. The life evaluation of these countries decreases gradually and not suddenly.

In contrast, Bulgaria, Estonia, Lithuania, Latvia, and Romania show an upward pattern. Their average life evaluation increases gradually over time, indicating steady improvement rather than sudden jumps.

Over time, countries show clearly different long-term trajectories. The largest declines are concentrated in a group of Western countries, which begin the period at relatively high levels and gradually lose ground across the years.

The most dramatic and sustained improvements can be seen in a portion of Eastern Europe, where life evaluation continues to increase steadily from low starting points. Over time, this trend helps to close a portion of these previously existing gaps.

A third cohort is stable, maintaining high levels of life evaluation with minimal fluctuations over time.

Over time, starting position proves less decisive than consistency. Some countries start high and remain high, while others start high and gradually fall. A few manage to improve sufficiently to change their relative position.

So, how does life feel across Europe?

What makes a country feel good to live in is not something that can be represented by a simple number. While rankings can give a sense of broad trends, they can also obscure important details. Life evaluation is the result of a dynamic interplay between economic resources, social and institutional arrangements, and the stability and/or erosion of those arrangements.

A superficial examination of Europe seems to indicate a familiar relationship: more income, better well-being. But this does not always yield the expected outcomes. A comparison of countries with similar economic resources can have vastly different lived experiences, indicating that economic resources are not the whole story.

Examining the various conditions that contribute to well-being can give a better sense of why this might be the case. While income offers a sense of economic stability, social support, health, freedom, and so on, all contribute to how that stability feels. They are not additive, and they interact in ways that can have similar outcomes, even if they are arrived at differently.

If we look across time, we can see that well-being levels do not remain static, but they also do not fluctuate wildly. Rather, they shift slowly and irregularly, with some countries holding steady, others steadily rising, and a few steadily falling.

Together, these findings support the idea that well-being is a systemic outcome. It is produced by the interplay of material conditions, social relationships, the reliability of institutions, and people’s sense of agency in their everyday lives. No one factor is paramount everywhere, and there is no singular way to get there.

Knowing what makes a country feel good, however, requires moving beyond rankings. It requires looking at the conditions of everyday life, how a society copes with stress and uncertainty, and whether people trust the institutions that guide their choices. Well-being, in other words, is not so much about good conditions as it is about whether those conditions hold up under stress.
